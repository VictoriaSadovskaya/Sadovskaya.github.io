{\Large

{ \bf {Newton's method for approximating  zeros of functions}} 

\vskip.5cm

{\large

\vskip.1cm
Let $g$ be a continuously differentiable function on $\R$. 
\vskip.1cm
Suppose that $\,\,g(x_*)=0$,\,\,\, $g'(x_*)\ne 0$,\,\,\,  and  $x_0$ is sufficiently close to $x_*$.\, 
\vskip.2cm
For $n\ge 0$,\,\, let   \,$x_{n+1}= x_n\,-$\,{\Large $\frac {g(x_n)}{g'(x_n)}$},\, \, that is 
\vskip.2cm
\hskip1cm $\,\,x_{n+1}=F(x_n)$,\,\, where $\,F(x)= x\,-$\,{\Large $\frac {g(x)}{g'(x)}$}.

\vskip.35cm

 Then the sequence   $\,x_n=F^n(x_0)$ converges to $x_*$.
\vskip.5cm 

$\bullet$ \, The same applies to a function $g:\C\to \C$.

\vskip.6cm 

\underline{Question.} \,  Given a zero $z_*$ of $g$,\, what does the set of numbers 
\vskip.1cm
\hskip2.6cm $z\in \C\,$ such that $\,F^n(z)\to z_*\, $ looks like?




}
